About T-20 World Cup

T-20 World Cup is one of the three formats of cricket. As the name suggests, it is a 20-over match, and each bowler can bowl a maximum of 4 overs. It was introduced by the ECB in 2003. Each team has one innings of 20 overs, making matches last about 3 hours. This format was made to attract a wider audience by shortening the game.

The format gained global popularity and is now played both internationally and domestically by all major cricket-playing nations.

History of T-20 Cricket

Origin

When the Benson & Hedges Cup ended in 2002, the ECB wanted a format that could attract younger fans. Stuart Robertson proposed a 20-over-per-side format, which counties approved.

The first official Twenty20 matches were played on 13 June 2003. The format was a success, especially with high crowd attendance, like the Lord’s match in 2004 that drew over 27,000 people.

Worldwide Spread

Pakistan held its first domestic T20 tournament in 2004. Australia followed in 2005 with a full-house crowd of 20,000. The West Indies launched the Stanford 20/20 in 2006, heavily backed by billionaire Allen Stanford.

In 2007, large crowds continued for domestic and international matches. Notably, over 85,000 fans attended the 2008 Australia vs India T20 match at the MCG.

The Stanford Super Series featured high-stakes matches but ended in controversy when Allen Stanford was charged with fraud in 2009.
